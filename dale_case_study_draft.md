# From Hodgepodge to High Confidence: How Oklahoma Districts Are Solving Financial Literacy With PFL Academy

**A case study on standards-aligned curriculum adoption in Dale Public Schools — and what educators across the state are reporting.**

---

## The Problem: Scrambling for Standards, Scrambling for Speakers

When Oklahoma mandated personal financial literacy as a graduation requirement, districts didn't get a playbook. They got a deadline.

For schools like Dale Public Schools — a small district southeast of Oklahoma City — that meant teachers were suddenly responsible for a subject most had never been trained to teach. The result was predictable: a hodgepodge of borrowed worksheets, one-off guest speakers, and improvised lesson plans stitched together with good intentions but no cohesion.

The problems stacked up fast:

- **No standards alignment.** Teachers pulled from whatever they could find — a bank pamphlet here, a YouTube video there — with no way to verify coverage of Oklahoma's Personal Financial Literacy standards.
- **No ICAP/ILP compliance.** Oklahoma's Individual Career Academic Plan (ICAP) requirements demand documented, standards-aligned coursework that connects financial literacy to career readiness. Patchwork curricula couldn't demonstrate compliance.
- **Teacher burden.** Financial literacy fell to whoever had room in their schedule — counselors wearing three hats, career tech teachers absorbing an extra prep, veteran teachers learning a new subject from scratch.
- **Guest speaker dependency.** The traditional model of bringing in a banker or insurance agent to "cover" financial topics broke down immediately for any school operating outside standard hours. Evening programs, rural districts, and alternative education settings were left with nothing.

This wasn't unique to Dale. Across Oklahoma, districts were hitting the same wall. As Casey Perez, an Alternative Education Counselor/Director/Capstone Teacher at Clinton Public Schools, describes it:

> *"This is also the first year that we've had a packaged program for financial literacy. It's been kind of hodgepodged before."*

For Perez — who simultaneously serves as counselor, director, and capstone teacher in Clinton's evening alternative education program (classes run 4–8 PM) — the guest speaker model was especially broken:

> *"Because we were a nighttime program, it was difficult for us to get the people that would come in during the day — the insurance agent, the person from the bank. Our classes are from 4 to 8 in the evenings... it's hard to get people to come in. It helped me not having to scramble to find somebody who was willing to come in at night."*

The status quo wasn't just inconvenient. It was failing students.

## The Implementation: How It Actually Works in Oklahoma Classrooms

### Dale Public Schools

Dianna Wilkins, a 12-year veteran teacher at Dale High School, adopted PFL Academy as the district's financial literacy platform. As Dale's first implementation, the adoption demonstrated that the curriculum could work in a traditional high school setting — with standards alignment, dashboard tracking, and teacher guide support providing the structure that the previous patchwork approach lacked.

### Across Oklahoma Districts: Clinton Public Schools

At Clinton Public Schools, Casey Perez runs PFL Academy in an environment that tests every assumption about curriculum delivery: evening alternative education classes, students with widely varying paces, and a teacher who's simultaneously a counselor, director, and capstone instructor.

**Synchronous delivery with built-in flexibility.** Perez delivers PFL Academy synchronously — the class moves through standards together, with structured discussion after activities. But the platform flexes when it needs to:

> *"Plus, when I need it to be, they are able to go off on their own and do their own and then we can come back together later and like, 'Okay, we missed this talking about this together. Let's talk about it now.' And it doesn't make them wait on me whenever for some reason I can't be there in that capacity because I have three different roles and sometimes one has to take priority over the other."*

**Sub-ready by design.** Perez frequently has to step out of the classroom for his other responsibilities. An additional staff member can step in seamlessly:

> *"She knows that if I say, 'Okay, we're working on this,' she knows exactly what I'm talking about. So it's made it easier for me to be able to step out and take care of other business when I need to."*

**Dashboard visibility.** The teacher dashboard provides both macro and micro views:

> *"I liked being able to go in and look at specific students, but then also look at the whole class and figure out what is it that maybe we need to go back and touch on."*

**Platform iteration.** Perez has seen the platform improve in real time between semesters:

> *"From last semester to this semester, it is easier to figure out how to do some things... being able to reset it pretty quick and easy while I was basically on the run — it was perfect."*

## The Results: What Changed

### Students are leading discussions, not sitting through them.

The most consistent signal from Oklahoma classrooms: students are actively engaged in financial conversations in ways they aren't in other subjects.

Perez describes what happens when students complete an activity before the class discussion:

> *"Having a discussion — they take over that discussion. I don't have to prompt them a whole lot. So they're getting that information that I would not have been able to teach as well because this would be my first year... but the kids are definitely getting it and they're showing me just in their conversation and the discussions that we have that they are understanding it."*

And this from a population of alternative education students — kids who, by definition, haven't thrived in traditional classroom settings:

> *"I haven't heard a lot of these kids socialize as much as they have before over something that's not art. So it's pretty interesting that it's about financial literacy."*

### Students are applying what they learn — immediately.

The proof isn't in test scores. It's in what students do after class.

One student wanted to buy his late aunt's house. He skipped ahead to the home loan standard, went through the material, then walked into a bank:

> *"When they went to talk to the bank, they knew what they were talking about. He came back and told me — that was amazing. I didn't feel intimidated by anything. I felt like I understood what was going on."*

Another student researching a car purchase asked to jump to the auto loan standard to make sure he was getting a fair deal.

These aren't hypothetical outcomes. These are 18- and 19-year-olds navigating real financial decisions with confidence — because the curriculum prepared them for exactly these conversations.

### Military-bound students are completing coursework on an accelerated timeline.

For students joining the military early, PFL Academy's structured, self-paced capability means they can complete their financial literacy requirement with quality — not just speed — so their recruiters get confirmation and they ship to boot camp on schedule.

> *"We're trying to get them through as fast as we can — and still with quality — so that when they can be completely finished, we can let their recruiter know and they can get them sent to boot camp. Big life changes right there."*

### Teachers are recommending PFL Academy without being asked.

When asked what he'd tell a teacher who's never taught financial literacy:

> *"Oh my gosh, highly recommended. It's in such an understandable way for the teacher and the student. It drives questions that you wouldn't even think about with the students when they become engaged. It's easier than anything that I've seen and that I've looked at. But it's also quality — and that's the thing that I want my kids to have."*

## The ICAP/ILP Moat: Why This Matters Beyond Financial Literacy

Oklahoma requires students to complete an Individual Career Academic Plan (ICAP). Financial literacy is one component. Career readiness documentation — resumes, mock interviews, capstone projects — is another.

Most financial literacy providers stop at financial literacy. Districts then need a *separate* system for career readiness documentation, a *separate* tool for mock interviews, a *separate* process for capstone project tracking.

PFL Academy's Standard 15 integrates all of this:

- **Resume builders** tied to career readiness standards
- **AI-powered mock interview simulation** with video recording, automated feedback on body language and responses, and scoring
- **Capstone project framework** aligned to state requirements

For districts — especially those with counselors wearing multiple hats (like Perez, who is literally the counselor, director, AND capstone teacher) — having ICAP/ILP compliance integrated into the same platform as financial literacy instruction isn't a nice-to-have. It's the difference between manageable and impossible.

This integration doesn't exist anywhere else. Not in NGPF (free but no ICAP). Not in Everfi (broad but shallow). Not in Banzai (gamified, no career readiness). Not in Ramsey Solutions (brand recognition, no state-specific alignment, no ICAP).

**PFL Academy is the only platform where financial literacy and ICAP/ILP compliance live in the same system.**
